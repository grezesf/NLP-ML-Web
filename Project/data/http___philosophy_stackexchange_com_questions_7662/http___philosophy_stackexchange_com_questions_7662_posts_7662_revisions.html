Why does Bernard Wiliams Williams need to insist that Shame in the Greek world is compatible with indignation?

Martha Nussbaum, in The Fragility of Good reflects on the discussion by Bernard Williams in Shame & Neccesity Necessity on the ending of Sophocles TrachiniaiTrachiniae where Heracles is stricken by agony on account of a centaurs centaur's malice. Watching him suffer, his son Hyllus, asks for suggnomusune, fellow-feeling, from the human-onlookers and contrasts this sympathy with the callousness of the gods:

You see the great indifference (agnomusune) of the gods

to to these things that have happened

who who begat us and are called our fathers

and and look on such sufferings.

What What is to come no one can see,

but but what is here now is pitiable for us

and and shameful for them

Nussbaum comments, on the characterisation of the gods as parents who sat by while terrible events occurred:

Obviously enough, the Greek family would view as profoundly reprehensible the conduct of any father who allowed such things to happen to his children when he could have stopped it. Such conduct is indeed shameful, and, as Williams has insisted, shame in the Greek world is compatible with moral reactions of indignation and blame.

Why does Williams have to insist that shame is compatible with indignation & blame? Is it generally understood that shame is not a moral reaction, and hence the incompatibility that Williams is insisting against? Or is it that the Greek world is a shame-culture incompatible with the Judeo-Christian blame-guilt complex?

(I'm guessing that Sophocles would be labelled as a Stoic - which is why hence the Stoic label).
