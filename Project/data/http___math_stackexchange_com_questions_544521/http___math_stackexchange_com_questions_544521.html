Homogeneous ideals

up vote 0 down vote favorite
1

a) Let $I$ be a homogeneous ideal of $A[x]$. Then $I$ is a prime ideal if and only if for every two homogeneous functions $f, g ∈ A[x]$ such that $fg ∈ I$, either $f ∈ I$, or $g ∈ I$.

Let $f, g ∈ A[x]$ such that $fg ∈ I$ suppose by contradiction then $f$ and $g$ is not in $I$, let $f = \sum_{d=0}^m f^{[d]}$ and $g = \sum_{d=0}^s g^{[d]}$ without loss of generality, we may assume that neither $f^{[m]}$ nor $g^{[s]}$ is in I (otherwise we can consider $f - f^{[m]}$ or $g - g^{[s]}$ instead). Then $f^{[m]}g^{[s]} = fg^{[ms]}$ is an element of I. Hence $f^{[m]}$ or $g^{[s]}$ is in I this give a contradiction.

The other way is clear.

Is it correct my prove or there are a more simple way to prove this?

b) Let $I$ be an homogeneous ideal of $A[x]$. Then the annihilator (ann($I$)) of $I$ is a homogeneous ideal.

I can not prove this, how can I prove it?
