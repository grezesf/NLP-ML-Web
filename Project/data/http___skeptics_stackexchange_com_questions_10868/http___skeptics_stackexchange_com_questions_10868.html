What was the Earth rotation when it was formed? [closed]

up vote 1 down vote favorite

In an answer to another question, it was claimed that:

The magnitude of tidal friction depends on the arrangement of the continents. In the past, the continents were arranged such that tidal friction, and thus the rates of earth's slowing and the moon's recession, would have been less. The earth's rotation has slowed at a rate of two seconds every 100,000 years (Eicher 1976).

So let's try to naively calculate the length of the day when the Earth formed:

Today 1 day = 24 hours = 24 * 60 * 60 seconds = 86400 seconds.

2 seconds every 100,000 years in 4.6 billions years = 2 * 4,600,000,000 / 100,000 = 92,000 seconds.

This means that the day is 92000 longer today than when the Earth was formed, which means that when the Earth formed the rotation was 86400 - 92000 = -5600

So we "conclude" that the length of the day when the Earth was created was -5600 seconds. However, this conclusion is obviously non-sense, because negative time does not exist. Even if it was a small positive number it would be erroneous, as this would imply an extreme centrifugal force that would tear the planet apart.

This calculation was too naive. One should consider that the planet deceleration is not expected to be constant, but will converge to zero over time. This would mean that rotation decelerated faster in the past. However, this makes the calculation yet more absurd by giving a still yet more negative number.

So, what is wrong?

Is the 2 seconds in 100,000 years reasonably accurate? (Since these numbers are far too simplistic and the citation is somewhat old, I think not). If not accurate, what it is instead?

We should not neglect the Moon formation big-splash. Could this simple calculation be evolved into an evidence that after the violent Moon-forming big-splash the Earth got a very-very fast rotation induced by an inertial mechanical push from Theia?

And finally, what was the day duration when the Earth formed (or at least when it recovered it round form after the Theia collision)?

According to this University of Illinois Physics Department website:

The Earth's rotation rate slows at a rate of 0.005 seconds per year per year. This is enough to cause havoc with calculations of when and where solar eclipses were visible in ancient times. If the day is constant in length, then a different part of the Earth would be under the moon's shadow than if it were slowing down. The accumulated time difference is a few hours over the course of a couple of thousand years. Over the 4.6 billion year life of the Earth, this means that the day started out about 14 hours long.
