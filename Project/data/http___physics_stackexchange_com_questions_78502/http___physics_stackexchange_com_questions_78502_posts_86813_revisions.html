The Cauchy stress matrix $\Sigma$ is a $3 \times 3$ real symmetric matrix. It is interesting that we may without problems generalize $\Sigma$ to a $3 \times 3$ Hermitian matrix. It has three mutually orthogonal principal stress directions with principal stresses (eigenvalues) $\lambda_1\geq \lambda_2\geq $\lambda_1\geq\lambda_2\geq \lambda_3$.

$$\tag{1} |n\rangle~=~\begin |{\bf n}\rangle~=~\begin{bmatrix} n_1\\ n_2\\ n_3\end{bmatrix}~\in~\mathbb{C}^3,$$

where $\langle n|n\rangle=1$ {\bf n}|{\bf n}\rangle=1$. Defined the directional stress as the "expectation value"

$$\tag{2} \sigma_n~ \sigma_{\bf n}~:=~E_n=~E_{\bf n}(\Sigma)~:=~ \langle n| {\bf n}| \Sigma |n\rangle |{\bf n}\rangle. $$

$$\tag{3} \tau_n~ \tau_{\bf n}~:=~\sqrt{{\rm Var}_n_{\bf n}(\Sigma)}  ~:=~\sqrt{\langle n| {\bf n}| \Sigma^2 |n\rangle |{\bf n}\rangle - \langle n|\langle {\bf n}| \Sigma |n\rangle^2 |{\bf n}\rangle^2} ~=~\sqrt{\langle n| {\bf n}| \Sigma^2 |n\rangle |{\bf n}\rangle - \sigma_n^2\sigma_{\bf n}^2}~\geq~0.$$

Let us now choose a coordinate system such that the $x$-axis is parallel to the unit vector $n$. In other words $\sigma_n$, the "expectation value" $\sigma_{\bf n}$ is then the first diagonal element in the $\Sigma$ matrix.

According to the Schur-Horn Theorem, a diagonal element $\sigma_n$ $\sigma_{\bf n}$ of $\Sigma$ must lie between the eigenvalues

$$\tag{4} \lambda_3~\leq~ \sigma_n~\leq~ \sigma_{\bf n}~\leq~ \lambda_1. $$

Equation (4) shows that the point $(\sigma_n\sigma_{\bf n},\tau_n\tau_{\bf n})$ must belong to a half-strip in the $(\sigma_n\sigma_{\bf n},\tau_n\tau_{\bf n})$ plane. A half-strip is not quite Mohr's three semicircles  with centers $(\frac{\lambda_i+\lambda_j}{2},0)$ and radii $\frac{|\lambda_i-\lambda_j|}{2}$ for $i\neq j$; but we can do better, cf. below.

$\uparrow$ Figure 1: Mohr's three circles in the $(\sigma_{\bf n},\tau_{\bf n})$ plane. Possible $(\sigma_{\bf n},\tau_{\bf n})$ points are within the green region. The principal stresses $\lambda_i$ are called $\sigma_i$.

Similarly according to the Schur-Horn Theorem, a diagonal element $\sigma_n^2+\tau_n^2$ $\sigma_{\bf n}^2+\tau_{\bf n}^2$ of the Hermitian matrix $\Sigma^2$ must lie between the eigenvalues

$$\tag{5} \min_ \min_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}\lambda_i^2~\leq~ \sigma_n^2+\tau_n^2~\leq \sigma_{\bf n}^2+\tau_n^2~\leq ~ \max_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}\lambda_i^2. $$

More generally, a diagonal element $(\sigma_n\sigma_{\bf n}-\lambda)^2+\tau_n^2$^2+\tau_{\bf n}^2$ of the Hermitian matrix

$$\tag{7} \min_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}(\lambda_i-\lambda)^2~\leq~ (\sigma_n\sigma_{\bf n}-\lambda)^2+\tau_n^2~\leq~\max_^2+\tau_{\bf n}^2~\leq~\max_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}(\lambda_i-\lambda)^2 , $$

Equation (7) leads to the three Mohr's semicircles if we choose $\lambda = \frac{\lambda_i+\lambda_j}{2}$ with $i\neq j$.

The three prongs that touch the horizontal $\sigma_{\bf n}$-axis can in a quantum mechanical analogy be understood as the uncertainty $\tau_{\bf n}$ can only become small if the unit vector $\bf n$ is close to one of the three principal stress directions.

The Cauchy stress matrix $\Sigma$ is a $3 \times 3$ real symmetric matrix. It is interesting that we may without problems generalize $\Sigma$ to a $3 \times 3$ Hermitian matrix. It has three mutually orthogonal principal stress directions with principal stresses (eigenvalues) $\lambda_1\geq \lambda_2\geq \lambda_3$.

Consider an arbitrary unit vector

$$\tag{1} |n\rangle~=~\begin{bmatrix} n_1\\ n_2\\ n_3\end{bmatrix}~\in~\mathbb{C}^3,$$

where $\langle n|n\rangle=1$. Defined the directional stress as the "expectation value"

$$\tag{2} \sigma_n~:=~E_n(\Sigma)~:=~ \langle n| \Sigma |n\rangle. $$

Let us define the unsigned shear-stress as the "standard deviation"

$$\tag{3} \tau_n~:=~\sqrt{{\rm Var}_n(\Sigma)}~:=~\sqrt{\langle n| \Sigma^2 |n\rangle- \langle n| \Sigma |n\rangle^2}~=~\sqrt{\langle n| \Sigma^2 |n\rangle- \sigma_n^2}~\geq~0.$$

Let us now choose a coordinate system such that the $x$-axis is parallel to the unit vector $n$. In other words $\sigma_n$ is the first diagonal element in the $\Sigma$ matrix.

According to the Schur-Horn Theorem, a diagonal element $\sigma_n$ of $\Sigma$ must lie between the eigenvalues

$$\tag{4} \lambda_3~\leq~ \sigma_n~\leq~ \lambda_1. $$

The fact that diagonal elements of a Hermitian matrix always are constrained by its eigenvalues has profound consequences in many areas of physics, see e.g. this Phys.SE post.

Equation (4) shows that the point $(\sigma_n,\tau_n)$ must belong to a half-strip in the $(\sigma_n,\tau_n)$ plane. A half-strip is not quite Mohr's semicircles, but we can do better, cf. below.

Similarly according to the Schur-Horn Theorem, a diagonal element $\sigma_n^2+\tau_n^2$ of the Hermitian matrix $\Sigma^2$ must lie between the eigenvalues

$$\tag{5} \min_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}\lambda_i^2~\leq~ \sigma_n^2+\tau_n^2~\leq ~ \max_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}\lambda_i^2. $$

More generally, a diagonal element $(\sigma_n-\lambda)^2+\tau_n^2$ of the Hermitian matrix

$$\tag{6} (\Sigma-\lambda{\bf 1}_{3\times 3})^2 ~=~\Sigma^2-2\lambda\Sigma+\lambda^2{\bf 1}_{3\times 3} $$

must lie between the eigenvalues

$$\tag{7} \min_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}(\lambda_i-\lambda)^2~\leq~ (\sigma_n-\lambda)^2+\tau_n^2~\leq~\max_{i\in\{1,2,3\}}(\lambda_i-\lambda)^2 , $$

for a real parameter $\lambda\in \mathbb{R}$.

Equation (7) leads to the three Mohr's semicircles if we choose $\lambda = \frac{\lambda_i+\lambda_j}{2}$ with $i\neq j$.
